Case Study: The Difficult Employee

Note: all mediations are confidential. This case study is a composite of several mediations.

The Situation

Richard, a manager in his company's IT division, was starting to have problems with one of his team members, Lorraine. Her engagement with her work and the company was dwindling. Richard was fielding angry calls from the client she was working with, dealing with criticism from his manager, and trying to reengage Lorraine. Lorraine had been an excellent team member when she transferred into his team. What was going wrong?

How the Problem Started

Lorraine had been working at the company for four years. Since joining Richard's team six months ago, Lorraine had been transitioning effectively into being customer-facing. Besides having top-notch programming skills, she proposed an app the team could develop to provide value to the company's customers. And given her project-management experience, Richard knew she could handle a difficult internal client from another office.

But after a few months, Lorraine's performance began to suffer. She missed deadlines for fixing bugs, and was slow to check her code back in. She declared she had no more ideas for the app she suggested. And she could be heard having angry calls with the client.

The Problem Affects the Entire Team

Lorraine's increasingly short temper affected the entire team. Work quality suffered, and engagement was deteriorating. Richard's manager was warning him to get his team straightened out. He showed Richard some figures representing the cost to the company in lost productivity and lower quality of work. And that didn't even take into account the extra stress on Richard.

The Manager Proposes a Novel Solution

Richard knew that a colleague of his at another company had solved a similar problem there, by calling in a mediator to coach employees through a process of restoring productivity and engagement. So he went to HR and suggested hiring a mediator to work with Lorraine. It would be less expensive than hiring and on-boarding someone new. Also, Lorraine had been a valued employee for the four years prior to joining Richard's team.

I Get a Call from HR

When a team member from HR in Richard's company called me, I called Richard back that day. We worked with HR to schedule time for an on-site session where I could coach Richard and Lorraine through the process of resolving the difficulties between them.

We agreed that the mediation would be confidential (I require that for my work), and that no details of the mediation would appear in Lorraine's personnel file.

I Coach Richard and Lorraine to Resolve the Issues between Them

The mediation started with both Richard and Lorraine telling me their perspective on the problems they were having working together. Through a combination of coaching them in problem-solving conversation with each other, and working with each of them privately, I helped them surface some key issues.

The key issue was that Lorraine hadn't been told just how difficult the client was to work with. With careful coaching from me, Richard was able to acknowledge that he hadn't been candid with Lorraine, because he hadn't wanted to discourage her, as he put it. So Lorraine was blindsided by a client whose MO was to be effusively friendly and pleasant at the start, but — once she'd lulled her victim into a false sense of security — she was a bully, unreasonable, demanding, and demoralizing.

Another key issue was Richard's extreme discomfort with conflict of any kind, which led him to try to "nice" away anything remotely unpleasant in any conversation he had.

A third key issue was that Lorraine was scared she would lose her job if she could work with this client. Her fear was coming out in the form of anger and unprofessionalism.

The Results of the Coaching/Mediation Session

I coached them in creating a list of specific actionable items each of them could implement. These action items included techniques for Richard to be more at ease with disagreement, and strategies for Lorraine to use to stand up to the client's bullying. Lorraine and Richard also agreed that Richard would join all phone calls with the client from then on. Together, they came up with a timetable for Lorraine's work to be back to the level of excellence it was when she first joined Richard's team.

Before the mediation ended, Richard told us he was glad that Lorraine had mentioned the effects his discomfort with conflict were having on her and on the team. He'd noticed that he wasn't as effective with his team as he wanted to be, and now he had some actions he could take to improve.

The Results

When I followed up with Richard a month later, he reported that Lorraine was highly engaged with the company again, and the client was delighted and actually started praising Richard and his team to the C-suite. The app Lorraine had suggested had been pitched to the business side with great success, and promised to be a major part of strategic engagement with their customers.

Richard had taken what he'd learned about himself during the mediation, and used it to become a better, more effective manager.

Richard got kudos from his manager for re-engaging a valuable employee, and saving the company the expense of hiring on-boarding someone new. Richard knew that his team was on track for great success on into the future.
